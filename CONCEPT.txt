D&D SESSION APP — EXPANDED CONCEPT
==================================

CORE PREMISE
------------
This app is a shared digital space for Game Masters and players to create
campaigns, prepare scenes, and run Dungeons & Dragons sessions.

Its defining principle is CONTROLLED SIMPLICITY:

    Players should only see the information and controls that matter to them
    at that exact moment.

Many virtual tabletops place maps, character sheets, measurement tools,
drawing controls, menus, combat trackers, journals, macros, and configuration
panels on the screen simultaneously. This app takes the opposite approach.

The Game Master prepares and controls the world through Build Mode and GM
Tools, while players interact through a deliberately simplified Play Mode. The
interface changes depending on the type of scene being played.

The app contains two primary scene types:

  - Playing Fields, designed for roleplay, exploration, shopping, and
    storytelling.
  - Battle Fields, designed for tactical combat, dungeons, hazards, and
    interactive environments.

Campaigns contain these scenes alongside characters, enemies, images, notes,
items, and other reusable resources.


VERSION ONE — GENERAL IMPROVEMENT
=================================

PRODUCT VISION
--------------
The app should feel less like complicated tabletop software and more like a
purpose-built game that happens to be controlled by a Game Master.

The GM receives powerful creation and moderation tools, but players are
protected from unnecessary complexity. A player entering a tavern should not
see initiative controls, wall-editing tools, lighting settings, monster
statistics, or twenty buttons they cannot use. They should see the tavern, the
characters present, their own character, and any actions currently available
to them.

Every screen should answer one question:

    What does this person need to do right now?


USER ROLES
----------

GAME MASTER
The GM creates and controls the campaign world. They can:
  - Create, edit, duplicate, organise, and delete scenes.
  - Switch between Build Mode and Session Mode.
  - Control NPCs, monsters, environmental objects, lighting, music, and events.
  - Reveal information selectively.
  - Move players when a scene is GM-controlled.
  - Override rules and outcomes.
  - Pause, rewind, or alter combat.
  - Import or create custom enemies, items, spells, and images.
  - Save scene templates for reuse.
  - Prepare hidden content without exposing it to players.

The GM is never limited by automation. The app can calculate and suggest
outcomes, but the GM always has final authority.

PLAYERS
Players control their characters only when the current scene requires it.
They can:
  - View information their character would reasonably know.
  - Access a simplified character sheet.
  - Roll checks, saves, attacks, and damage.
  - Use abilities, spells, items, and equipment.
  - Control their token during tactical scenes.
  - Interact with shops and other designated interfaces.
  - Receive private messages, discoveries, or secret information from the GM.
  - Make choices when the GM presents prompts or decisions.

The player interface should avoid administrative controls and irrelevant
information.


SCENE TYPE ONE: PLAYING FIELD
-----------------------------
A Playing Field is a roleplay-focused scene. It acts as a visual
representation of a location rather than a fully simulated tactical
environment. Examples: a tavern, a marketplace, a royal court, a campsite, a
village street, a ship at sea, a festival, a wizard's workshop.

The purpose of a Playing Field is to make conversations and exploration easier
to follow without turning every movement into tactical gameplay.

GM CONTROL — the GM controls the physical presentation of the scene:
  - Move player and NPC tokens.
  - Change character expressions or poses.
  - Trigger animations.
  - Open doors or reveal new areas.
  - Start music and ambient sound.
  - Display dialogue portraits.
  - Highlight important objects.
  - Introduce or remove characters.
  - Trigger weather or time-of-day changes.
  - Transition between visual states of the location.
  - Present choices or interactive prompts.

Movement is intentionally GM-controlled because exact positioning is usually
unimportant during roleplay.

PLAYER EXPERIENCE — players primarily observe, roleplay, and make decisions.
Their interface might contain:
  - Their character portrait.
  - A compact character sheet button.
  - Available roleplay actions.
  - Dice-roll requests.
  - Private information from the GM.
  - A shop button when a merchant is available.
  - Temporary prompts such as "Inspect the statue" or "Choose a response."

SHOPS — a built-in Playing Field feature. When the GM marks an NPC or object
as a shop, players can open a simplified storefront containing only the items
available to them.
Players can: browse items, read descriptions, compare prices, purchase items,
sell items when allowed, add items directly to inventory, see whether they
meet an item's requirements.
The GM can: set prices and stock, apply discounts or markups, create limited
stock, hide special items, require persuasion checks, approve purchases
manually, allow bargaining, create different inventories for different
players, make items appear after certain events.

A purchase does not need to be automatic. The GM can choose between automatic
transactions, approval-based transactions, or entirely manual shopping.


SCENE TYPE TWO: BATTLE FIELD
----------------------------
A Battle Field is a tactical environment designed for combat, dungeons, traps,
and position-based gameplay. Unlike Playing Fields, players directly control
their characters. The goal is to make combat feel like a turn-based tactical
RPG while preserving the flexibility of tabletop roleplaying.

PLAYER CONTROL — during their turn, a player can:
  - Move their character.
  - Preview movement distance.
  - Select attacks, spells, items, and abilities.
  - View valid targets.
  - See estimated ranges and affected areas.
  - Interact with doors, levers, switches, containers, and environmental
    objects.
  - End their turn.
  - Ask the GM a question or request an unusual action.

The app extracts relevant data from the player's character sheet. Selecting a
sword attack could automatically display: valid targets, attack range, attack
bonus, damage dice, advantage or disadvantage, relevant active effects.

GAME MASTER CONTROL — the GM controls: enemies and allied NPCs, hidden
creatures, traps and environmental hazards, initiative order, scripted events,
reinforcements, dynamic terrain, doors, walls, switches, and mechanisms,
lighting and visibility, rule overrides, secret rolls and information.

The GM can also "play god": move any token, change health or conditions, force
or cancel an effect, add or remove initiative entries, undo an action, rewind
the battle to an earlier state, ignore an automated rule, declare a custom
outcome, temporarily control a player character, reveal or conceal any part of
the map.

Automation exists to make play faster, not to challenge the authority of the
GM.

INTERACTIVE ENVIRONMENTS — Battle Fields should support objects with real
gameplay effects: a lever that opens a gate, a pressure plate that activates a
trap, a switch that turns off magical barriers, a bridge that collapses after
taking damage, a torch that changes visibility, a crystal that powers nearby
enemies, a door requiring a key/spell/skill check, a rotating statue that
redirects a beam, a barrel that explodes when damaged, a platform that moves
between floors.

These interactions can be connected through a simple logic system, e.g.:

    Lever pulled -> Gate opens -> Alarm activates -> Guards enter after two
    rounds

The GM should be able to create this without programming. A visual
trigger-and-effect editor would allow them to connect objects, conditions,
delays, and outcomes.

WALLS, VISION, AND DYNAMIC LIGHTING — Battle Fields support real walls and
line-of-sight calculations. The app can determine: what each character can
see, whether an enemy is hidden, whether a target is behind cover, how far
different light sources reach, which parts of the map remain unexplored,
whether darkness/fog/magical effects block vision. Players should see the
world from their own character's perspective rather than sharing one universal
view. The GM can disable or simplify these systems.


CHARACTER SHEETS
----------------
Character sheets are integrated into the app rather than treated as separate
documents. The app uses sheet data to generate context-sensitive actions.

A complete sheet may contain: ability scores; skills and saving throws; health
and temporary health; armour class; movement types; attacks; spells; features
and traits; conditions; equipment; inventory; currency; resources such as
spell slots or class points; notes, personality traits, ideals, bonds, and
flaws.

Players do not need to view the entire sheet constantly. During combat, they
see combat actions. During shopping, they see inventory and money. During a
social check, they see relevant skills.


CAMPAIGNS
---------
A campaign is the main organisational container. It includes: Playing Fields,
Battle Fields, player characters, NPCs, enemies, items, shops, images, music
and sound effects, notes, handouts, lore entries, custom rules, session
history, saved templates.

Campaign content can be arranged into folders such as: locations, chapters,
encounters, characters, monsters, items, unused ideas, completed scenes.

A campaign also remembers its current state. When the session ends, the app
saves where characters are located, which enemies are alive, which doors are
open, what items were purchased, and which events have occurred.


SESSION FLOW
------------
A typical session:
  1. The GM opens the campaign.
  2. Players join through a campaign code or invitation.
  3. The GM begins in a Playing Field representing a tavern.
  4. Players roleplay while the GM controls movement and NPCs.
  5. A player opens the shop interface and purchases supplies.
  6. The GM transitions the group to a forest Playing Field.
  7. An ambush begins.
  8. The GM converts or transitions to a prepared Battle Field.
  9. Players gain direct control of their tokens.
 10. Combat runs through the tactical interface.
 11. The GM triggers reinforcements and opens a hidden path.
 12. The battle ends.
 13. The GM returns control to a Playing Field.
 14. The campaign state is automatically saved.

The transition between roleplay and combat should feel seamless.
